    •   War correspondents and journalists

      War correspondents and journalists often get themselves into very dangerous situations while trying to find news coverage for the nobility of bringing the truth to the public. They are hoping to be in the right moment and in the right place to get a chance at the Pulitzer, instead of getting shot. Who doesn't remember hearing about journalists who were kidnapped and killed while doing coverage of the Middle East, for example? How about the newspaper's employees who were victims of attacks by cartels in Latin America? Far from the aura of fame of this profession, the reality is of survival in harsh conditions of nature, hoping not to get into trouble during a protest against a dictator.

      You can understand why, between war journalists, there is a frank competitive nature. There is also a strong competitiveness; of course, no one wants to be last to report the events of each day. Because they often stay in the same hotel, regardless of the TV channel they work for, it is very common to see them share taxis to the locations, share drinks in the bar after work, share some intelligence, share assistants and even share interviewees when the article isn't that important to their bosses back in the office. 

      These war journalists are often fluent in more than two exotic languages; with an acute sense of smell for the news and alert ears to read the political environment; a curious nature to find a talkative person to interview; and with lots of nerves to keep cool and calm in battle. The war correspondents are dynamic characters of the battle arena, and can certainly change the direction of a war with the facts they report to the global community. Journalists actually assist by sometimes saving lives and reporting the inhuman conditions of refugees, which helps the chances for the survival of the refugees.

      It is almost impossible to keep these people safe; there is no government in the world that can afford to assist each one of the reporters in their county. They basically have no refuge. They are there to cover the news.
