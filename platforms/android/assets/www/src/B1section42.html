    • Grammar

    •   Impersonal Verbs

      You already know there are some verbs with no subject in Spanish, but these verbs need a subject in English. These verbs always have an impersonal subject (it), they cannot have a personal one. 


      Some of these verbs are related to the weather: 

      - snow = it snows 

      - rain = it rains 

      - hail = it hails 

      - lighten = it lightnings 

      - freeze = it freezes 

      - thunder = it thunders 

      - drizzle = it drizzles 

      - thaw = it thaws 

      - dawn = it dawns 

      - get dark = it gets dark 


      We can also use some of these verbs as nouns with the same form: 

      - snow = the snow 

      - rain = the rain 

      - hail = the hail 

      - thunder = the thunder 

      - thunder = a thunderbolt 

      - thunder = a thunderclap 

      - drizzle = the drizzle 

      - thaw = the thaw 

      - dawn = the dawn 


      Some of them change their form to become nouns: 

      - to get dark = the darkness 

      - to freeze = the frost 

      - to lighten = the lightning 

      - a flash of lightning 

      - the flashes of lightning 


      There are some other verbs we can use with a personal and an impersonal subject: 

      look

      It looks dangerous Parece peligroso
      They look happy Parecen felices


      grow

      It grows quickly
      Crece rápido
      That child is growing very fast Ese niño está creciendo muy rápido


      happen
      It happened to me last year Me ocurrió el año pasado
      They happen to be near Aconteció que estaban cerca


      seem
      It seemed funny to me Me pareció divertido
      They seem to be right Parece que tienen razón


      begin
      It began at half past five Empezó a las 17:30
      I began a new lesson Empecé una nueva clase


      become
      It became true Se convirtió en realidad
      I became a lawyer Me convertí en abogada

    •   Hurricane Season in the Caribbean

      In the Caribbean, people have learned how to live with meteorological events, and they have formed a hurricane culture. In most of this region there is a routine that you learn as a child, because during your life in the tropical islands one must go through several tropical storms. People become an empiric weathermen, and can usually predict when will it rain and when will the sun will shine. During the hurricane season, everybody in the Caribbean is vigilant of the weather patterns by checking the internet; especially and constantly when the computer models from weather websites predict their island will have a cyclone.

      But the preparations to protect property are expensive and normally everybody waits to the last thirty-six hours when they are sure that the hurricane will hit in their community. It would be too expensive to do this routine many times during the season, and all for storms that may or may not arrive. This is typical life in the tropics and nobody panics. When they are sure that a hurricane is going to hit them, the preparations have to be finished in only thirty-six hours, quite a short time to take boats out of the water, protect the windows of homes and businesses, put plants and animals inside the house, etc. People are really anxious during those thirty-six hours because it is normally already raining and thundering; and people are angry at the storm, using bad names to refer to the hurricane. When hurricanes are large and dangerous they receive human names.

      During the hurricane, there is nothing to do but to wait, because it just rains cats and dogs; and it rains so bad that is dangerous to be located near the water, because the water could take your house down, so most Caribbean people do not build their houses on rivers. During the storm, it thunders and gales, along with the rain and the wind; any outdoor activity is impossible. Sometimes they cut the electricity during the worst hours for safety. It gets dark, but they restore the electricity later, so it's alright.

      Because it is important to be prepared and have ready the things that they need to endure this boring time, Caribbean people are always prepared in advance. There is no panic in Caribbean supermarkets; only the tourists panic. The locals are always ready with the hurricane kit: lots of water, candles for light, batteries for the radio, food and lots of liquor. They have to relax to be ready to clean up after the hurricane.
